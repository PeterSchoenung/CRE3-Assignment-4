1. Introduction

Real tubular reactors deviate from ideal plug flow behaviour due to backmixing — a phenomenon where fluid elements at different axial positions exchange mass with each other. This causes a distribution of residence times and affects reactor performance [1], [2].

Objectives

This assignment investigates backmixing in a tubular reactor using three approaches:

1.     Dispersion Model: solved as a boundary value problem (BVP) with Danckwerts boundary conditions

2.     Bo Number Analysis: investigating the effect of backmixing strength on conversion

3.     CSTR Cascade:  comparing a series of ideal CSTRs with the dispersion model

# ============================================
# PHASE 3 - STEP 4: BVP IMPLEMENTATION
# ============================================

# --- Dispersion Coefficient ---
Bo = 100                # Bodenstein number (start with 100)
D = u * L / Bo          # dispersion coefficient m²/s
print(f"Bodenstein Number Bo  : {Bo}")
print(f"Dispersion Coefficient: {D:.6f} m²/s")

# --- ODE Function for Dispersion Model ---
# Dispersion ODE: D*d²c/dz² - u*dc/dz - k*c = 0
# Rearranged    : d²c/dz² = (u/D)*dc/dz + (k/D)*c
# Let y[0] = c (concentration)
# Let y[1] = dc/dz (gradient)

def bvp_func(z, y):
    c    = y[0]          # concentration
    dcdz = y[1]          # concentration gradient

    # first derivative
    dy0 = dcdz

    # second derivative (from dispersion ODE)
    dy1 = (u/D)*dcdz + (k/D)*c

    return np.vstack((dy0, dy1))

# --- Danckwerts Boundary Conditions ---
# At z=0 (inlet): dc/dz = u/D * (c - c_A0)
# At z=L (outlet): dc/dz = 0

def bc_danckwerts(ya, yb):
    # ya = values at z=0 (inlet)
    # yb = values at z=L (outlet)

    bc_inlet  = ya[1] - (u/D)*(ya[0] - c_A0)  # Danckwerts inlet BC
    bc_outlet = yb[1]                           # zero gradient at outlet

    return np.array([bc_inlet, bc_outlet])

# --- Initial Guess for Solver ---
z = np.linspace(0, L, 100)       # axial positions from 0 to L
y_init      = np.zeros((2, z.size))
y_init[0,:] = c_A0               # guess: concentration starts at feed
y_init[1,:] = 0                  # guess: zero gradient

# --- Solve BVP ---
sol_bvp = solve_bvp(bvp_func, bc_danckwerts, z, y_init)

# --- Results ---
print(f"\nBVP Solver Successful       : {sol_bvp.success}")
print(f"Inlet concentration  (z=0) : {sol_bvp.y[0,0]:.4f} mol/m³")
print(f"Outlet concentration (z=L) : {sol_bvp.y[0,-1]:.4f} mol/m³")
conversion_bvp = (1 - sol_bvp.y[0,-1]/c_A0)*100
print(f"Conversion                 : {conversion_bvp:.2f}%")

# --- Plot BVP Result ---
fig = plt.figure(figsize=(8,4), dpi=150)
plt.plot(sol_bvp.x, sol_bvp.y[0,:],
         color="blue", linewidth=2,
         linestyle="-", label="BVP Solution")
plt.axhline(y=sol_bvp.y[0,-1],
            color='gray', linestyle='--',
            linewidth=1, label="Outlet Concentration")
plt.xlabel("Axial Position z / m", fontsize=12)
plt.ylabel("Concentration $c_A$ / mol m$^{-3}$", fontsize=12)
plt.title(f"Concentration Profile — Dispersion Model (Bo={Bo})", fontsize=12)
plt.xlim(0, L)
plt.ylim(0, c_A0*1.1)
plt.legend(fontsize=10)
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Task 2 Discussion

The results clearly show the effect that the Bodenstein number has on reactor performance, consistent with classical reactor theory [1], [2]. As the Bodenstein number increased from 10 to 10000, the conversion increased from 52.91% to 55.25%. This trend shows that by minimizing the axial dispersion in the reactor, fluid elements can flow through the reactor with less mixing in the axial direction and, thus, be more effective.

A large amount of backmixing of fresh reagents and partially reacted products takes place at low Bodenstein numbers. This mixing increases the residence time distribution and decreases the concentration driving force for the reaction, resulting in less overall conversion. When the Bodenstein number gets larger, the axial mixing gets smaller, and the reactor behaviour comes closer to that of an ideal plug flow reactor. As a result, a more homogeneous residence time of the reactants leads to increased conversions.

The difference in the conversion between Bo = 1000 (55.23%) and Bo = 10000 (55.25%) is negligible, suggesting that as the Bodenstein number increases, so does the improvement in the conversion. This indicates that, for Bodenstein numbers greater than about 1000, the reactor can be considered as an ideal plug flow reactor, and that there is not much practical advantage to be gained from further reduction in axial dispersion.

These observations are confirmed by comparison with the theoretical boundary cases. Of the two reactors, the ideal plug flow reactor had the highest conversion of 55.26% while the ideal continuously stirred tank reactor had a conversion of only 44.58%. The main difference between a plug flow reactor and a reactor that is not a plug flow reactor is that in the former the reactants are continuously exposed to higher concentrations along the length of the reactor and hence the reaction rate is higher. The opposite of this is a CSTR where the concentration is uniform throughout the reactor and the average reaction rate is less for a first-order reaction

On an industrial scale, it is not always feasible to obtain very high Bodenstein numbers due to the natural presence of turbulence, reactor geometry, flow maldistribution and manufacturing limitations, which cause an inevitable amount of axial dispersion. So, reactor design will try to reduce backmixing (where economically possible) without trying to completely remove it. These observations agree with industrial reactor behaviour reported in the literature [2], [5], [6].

Task 3 Discussion

The agreement between the dispersion model and cascade CSTR is very good for the range of operating conditions examined. The difference between conversion for Bo = 10 and N = 5 was about 0.35%, and for Bo = 20 and N = 10, the difference was reduced to about 0.10%. Both models yielded essentially the same conversions (54.97%) at Bo = 100 and N = 50, which is the validation of the relationship between Bo and N in both models.

As more CSTRs are added, the agreement gets better since a greater number of mixing stages has a more continuous concentration gradient, which is what is found in a tubular reactor. The concentration changes in only a few reactors in series are large, discrete steps. More stages cause these step changes to decrease towards the smooth profile predicted by the dispersion model.

The empirical relationship N ≈ Bo/2 was successfully confirmed by the simulation results.  This approximation has been widely used for preliminary reactor design and process simulation [2], [5].This relationship can be used practically to estimate the number of ideal CSTRs needed to represent a reactor with a given degree of axial dispersion. These approximations are very convenient for preliminary design of the reactor and process simulation where a detailed dispersion model may not be needed.

The dispersion model will typically be a more realistic model of an industrial tubular reactor as it explicitly includes the continuous axial mixing, although the CSTR cascade is a convenient approximation. However, the cascade model is useful in certain ways because of its simplicity and ease of implementation, particularly in the initial engineering calculations.

The results also show the concept of diminishing returns. The conversion was greatly enhanced by increasing the number of reactors from one to five, but very little further improvement was obtained with more reactors (twenty to fifty). This suggests that, after some threshold, the number of stages beyond that has little effect on the performance of the reactor, but it does make the modelling more complex. As such, the choice of the number of stages should be carefully considered to meet the accuracy requirement while still being efficient of computation.

# --- CSTR Cascade Function ---
def cstr_cascade(N, k, tau, c_A0):
    """
    Calculate concentration through N CSTRs in series
    N    = number of CSTRs
    k    = reaction rate constant (1/s)
    tau  = total residence time (s)
    c_A0 = feed concentration (mol/m³)
    """
    tau_n = tau / N          # residence time per stage
    c     = c_A0             # start with feed concentration

    concentrations = [c_A0]  # store all stage concentrations

    for i in range(N):
        c = c / (1 + k * tau_n)    # first order CSTR equation
        concentrations.append(c)

    return concentrations

6. Final Comparison and Conclusions

The effect of axial dispersion on the performance of a tubular reactor was investigated by using three complementary approaches: solving the dispersion model as a boundary value problem (BVP), validation using a backward initial value problem (IVP) integration, and comparison with an equivalent CSTR cascade. Combined these methods gave a complete evaluation of the effect of back mixing on concentration profiles and on reactor conversion.

The BVP solution converged successfully and was validated by using the backward IVP solution (both gave the same inlet concentrations and a zero numerical difference). This good agreement indicates that the numerical implementation was correct and that the governing equations and the Danckwerts boundary conditions were correctly implemented.

 

Different Bodenstein numbers were investigated and the results indicated that the conversions in the reactor improved with decreasing axial dispersion. The conversion improved from 52.91% at Bo = 10 to 55.25% at Bo = 10000 which is close to the ideal plug flow reactor value of 55.26%. The results show that decreasing the backmixing reduces the residence time distribution, which leads to better utilization of the reactants in the reactor and hence higher conversion. The small difference in the values of Bo=1000 and Bo=10000 also indicates that when dispersion reaches a certain level, further reducing it gives limited improvement.

The comparison of the dispersion model with the CSTR cascade, also indicated that the relationship N ≈ Bo/2 is an effective way to approximate the axial dispersion as a cascade of ideal reactors. For increasing numbers of CSTR stages, the cascade results became increasingly close to the concentration profiles and conversions as predicted by the dispersion model, showing that both models are consistent with current reactor engineering theory.

In general, the outcomes indicate that axial dispersion is measurable and affects tubular reactor performance and should be taken into account when designing and analyzing the reactors. Ideal plug flow behaviour ensures the maximum conversion in a first-order reaction, but is never achieved in an actual industrial reactor because there will always be some back mixing. Thus, the reactor designer/engineers have to trade off reactor performance with equipment cost and operation constraints in selecting an appropriate reactor design.
